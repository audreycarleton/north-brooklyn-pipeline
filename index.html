National Grid is building a pipeline through Brooklyn. Here's everything you need to know.

10,000 and counting: That’s how many New Yorkers have recently signed a petition against the 6,200-foot expansion of a pipeline running through predominantly Black and brown communities in North Brooklyn.

The Metropolitan Reliability Infrastructure (MRI) project, more commonly known as the ‘North Brooklyn Pipeline’ would carry fracked gas through Brownsville, Bedford-Stuyvesant, Bushwick, Williamsburg, and Greenpoint, to a National Grid Depot on Newtown Creek. Construction for the pipeline began in 2017 — the first three phases have been completed, the fourth is currently underway, and activists are fighting against the fifth, which National Grid has yet to receive approval on. The proposed fifth phase would cost $185-million to accomplish, and National Grid is hoping to receive state permission to hike New Yorkers’ tax rates to pay for it.

FrackTracker estimates show that approximately 153,000 New Yorkers reside within the evacuation zone of the pipeline, alongside 60 schools and 80 daycare centers, were it to leak or explode. But these at-risk residents are not those that are being served by the pipeline, many in opposition to it argue, including New York City Public Advocate Jumaane Williams. The pipeline is also at risk of leaking methane — a greenhouse gas that contributes an estimated 86 times more to atmospheric warming than carbon dioxide.
